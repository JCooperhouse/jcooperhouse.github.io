BlueNalu · Global Consumer & Chef Research

Understanding how the world will actually eat cell-cultured seafood.

A $250K, 8-market, 5-language program behind a category that didn't exist yet.

BlueNalu is pioneering cell-cultured seafood — a genuinely new category, with no off-the-shelf market data to lean on. Before the company could make decisions about which markets to enter, which species to lead with, and how to talk about the product, we needed to hear directly from the people who would eventually eat it and cook it.

The challenge

Build a credible global evidence base for a product most consumers had never heard of — across very different food cultures — without priming answers or overstating consumer enthusiasm.

The approach

I designed and managed a $250K, multi-phase program spanning eight markets and five languages. It combined large-scale quantitative surveys with 10,000+ consumers and 80+ in-depth chef and industry expert interviews. I worked closely with regional partners to get the translations and cultural framing right, and with internal marketing and R&D to make sure the questions mapped to decisions they actually needed to make.

The result

The findings became a cornerstone of BlueNalu's go-to-market thinking — shaping prioritization across regions, informing product and partnership conversations, and giving the exec team a shared evidence base in board and investor discussions. The chef interviews, in particular, gave culinary teams a grounded read on how cell-cultured seafood would need to behave in a professional kitchen to earn a spot on menus.

San Diego Asian Grocery · Market Intelligence

Mapping a San Diego grocery segment no one had mapped before.

Forty-four independent Asian grocers, ten neighborhoods, one clear picture of where the whitespace is.

I wanted to understand the Asian grocery landscape in San Diego County — a segment dominated by independents, underserved by syndicated data, and shaped heavily by neighborhood demographics. The question was deceptively simple: where are the real concentrations, where are the gaps, and what does that tell us about opportunity? I took it on independently to find out.

The challenge

Build a trustworthy, ground-truthed map of an independent-retailer segment that most market reports skip entirely — and do it at a level of detail that could actually inform a siting or partnership conversation.

The approach

I put together a county-wide audit of 44 stores across 10 neighborhoods, layering in demographics, corridor-level concentration, and cultural specialization by store. The analysis pulls apart the three distinct tiers the segment actually operates in — from the Convoy Street hub, to emerging corridors, to underserved pockets where demand outpaces supply.

Brighthelm · Play Kingdom Concept & Pitch

A family entertainment concept, pressure-tested against the unit economics.

Turning a play-space concept into a funded pitch, built on real demand and a defensible P&L.

Play Kingdom is a family entertainment concept I'm building independently, targeting the gap between fast-casual kids' activities and large, day-long destinations. I had the creative vision; what it needed next was a grounded case — one investors could interrogate line by line.

The challenge

Translate a creative concept into an investor-ready story: market demand, target audience, pricing, unit economics, and a path to breakeven that held up under scrutiny.

The approach

I sized local family-entertainment demand, benchmarked comparable venues, stress-tested pricing assumptions, and built a P&L that made the break-even math visible. The pitch narrative was built to feel human — a place families actually want to spend a Saturday — while the numbers underneath were built to survive a skeptical read.

The result

The pitch landed on an approximately $110K/month revenue model with a four-month path to breakeven in a 5,000 sq ft footprint, backed by the audience sizing and competitive benchmarks in the deck. More importantly, it gave me a narrative I can take any investor through with confidence.

Wandering in Paradise · Short-Form Video Case Study

Testing what makes a short-form video worth staying for.

Five shorts, five San Diego places, one honest study of what holds attention.

Wandering in Paradise is a personal short-form video project built around San Diego places — a running experiment in how framing, pacing, and on-camera presence shape completion rates and audience response.

The approach

Each short is a small test: different hooks, different cuts, different ways of showing up on camera. I track what lands — not just views, but watch-through, saves, and comments — and carry the lessons into the next one. It's the same instinct I bring to consumer research: form a hypothesis, watch real people interact with it, adjust, and try again.

About

A little about how I work.

I came up in consumer insights the practical way — running real studies for real decisions, learning what makes a finding land with a team and what makes one quietly get shelved.

At BlueNalu, I led global research programs across eight markets and five languages, working closely with marketing, R&D, and exec stakeholders to turn complex consumer data into clear direction.
